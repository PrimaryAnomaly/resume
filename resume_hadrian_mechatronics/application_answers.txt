Why are you applying? What excites you about the opportunity?

I've spent my career designing and building electromechanical systems that have to work in the hardest environments, satellite payloads with pressurized fluidics and thermal management, ground support equipment for military satellite integration, embedded sensor platforms that run autonomously for days. What excites me about Hadrian is the chance to apply that same kind of hands-on systems thinking to factory-scale automation for aerospace and defense. Designing end-of-arm tooling, building out automation cells, commissioning robotic systems on a real factory floor, that's the work I want to be doing. The fact that Hadrian is building these systems from scratch rather than retrofitting legacy lines means there's real engineering ownership and room to make first-principles design decisions. That's where I thrive.

What's the most relevant project you've worked on that makes you a great fit for this role?

During my PhD I designed and built a complete biological CubeSat payload system from concept through validated hardware. That meant custom mechanism design for pressurized fluidic actuation, thermal management subsystems, multi-modal sensor integration with custom PCBs, and embedded firmware for autonomous real-time control. I owned every layer of the system, mechanical assemblies, electrical integration, firmware, and system-level validation including environmental testing. I applied DFM and tolerance analysis to transition designs from early prototypes to production-ready hardware, and I did structured root cause analysis when things broke. At Hanwha Systems I then scaled that skillset to full satellite programs, designing EGSE and automation cell configurations, leading electromechanical integration campaigns, and commissioning systems through acceptance testing. That combination of ground-up electromechanical design and production-scale integration is directly relevant to building and deploying automation systems at Hadrian.
